Magnon Resilience in Diluted Antiferromagnets

Y-doped TbSb · Crystal-field hybridization · Inelastic neutron scattering

Overview

TbSb is a rock-salt rare-earth antiferromagnet that orders below TN ≈ 15 K. Its Tb³⁺ ions carry large moments and sit in a strong cubic crystal field from the surrounding Sb cage, which splits the J = 6 ground multiplet into a clean, well-characterized level scheme. Swap non-magnetic yttrium onto the terbium site and the magnetic sublattice thins out; percolation theory says that below the percolation threshold, long-range order and coherent spin waves have to give way.

They don't — or at least not where they should. Measuring the magnon spectrum across a series of (Tb1−xYx)Sb samples with inelastic neutron scattering, I find well-defined magnon branches persisting at dilutions where the simple models predict collapse. The best explanation so far is crystal-field hybridization: mixing between the single-ion levels and the collective modes appears to be what stabilizes the magnons against dilution.

Experimental Approach

  • Inelastic neutron scattering on powder and single-crystal (Tb1−xYx)Sb samples at HYSPEC, ARCS, and VERITAS (SNS, ORNL)
  • Three-dimensional magnetic pair distribution function analysis at CORELLI (SNS, ORNL)
  • Elastic neutron diffraction to determine the magnetic order parameter across the doping series
  • Physical property measurements (PPMS/MPMS) to map the magnetic phase boundaries
  • Crystal-field modeling to isolate single-ion contributions to the magnon stabilization

Central Questions

  • At what yttrium concentration do coherent magnon branches break down, and how does this compare to the classical percolation threshold?
  • What is the quantitative role of crystal-field hybridization in protecting magnon coherence against magnetic dilution?
  • How does thermal population of excited crystal-field levels interact with the diluted spin network to modify the magnon dispersion?
  • Can this resilience mechanism be generalized to other rare-earth antiferromagnets with strong single-ion anisotropy?

TbSb as a Model System

What makes TbSb such a good testbed is that its single-ion physics holds no surprises. The Tb³⁺ crystal-field parameters in the cubic Sb environment were pinned down long ago by optical and neutron spectroscopy, the parent compound's magnon dispersion is cleanly resolved, and Y³⁺ slots in for Tb³⁺ without distorting the lattice or adding charge disorder. So when the system strays from percolation behavior, the deviation points straight at the crystal-field coupling — there is no competing structural or electronic suspect.

That is the advantage over more complicated diluted magnets, where several mechanisms operate at once and scattering data alone cannot pull them apart. TbSb turns a messy general question — does single-ion anisotropy protect collective magnetism in a disordered system? — into a clean, controlled experiment.

Crystal-Field Hybridization and Magnon Stability

The crystal field splits a rare-earth ion's ground multiplet into discrete levels. When the gap between the ground state and the excited levels is comparable to the magnon bandwidth, the two kinds of excitation cannot stay independent: they hybridize into mixed magnon–crystal-field (magnon-CEF) modes. The effect is genuinely quantum mechanical — it has no classical counterpart — and it can reshape the magnon dispersion, lifetime, and gap substantially.

Dilution complicates this in an interesting way. Each Tb³⁺ site sees a local environment set by its particular Y/Tb neighborhood, so the crystal-field splittings acquire a distribution across the sample. Our measurements ask how that distributed coupling feeds back into the collective spectrum: does the hybridization pin the magnon branches in place or smear them out — and how does the answer change with doping and temperature?

Broader Significance

When do collective spin excitations survive disorder, and why? The question sounds academic but has practical teeth. Magnon coherence length controls thermal conductivity in magnetic insulators, sets how far spin waves travel in magnonic devices, and limits the fidelity of quantum correlations in candidate quantum materials. Working out when dilution destroys magnon coherence — and naming the mechanism when it doesn't — gives real design rules for magnets that tolerate disorder.
