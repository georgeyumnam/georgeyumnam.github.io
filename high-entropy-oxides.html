High-Entropy Oxides

Chemical disorder · Frustrated magnetism · Neutron & X-ray total scattering

Overview

High-entropy oxides (HEOs) are a recently discovered class of single-phase materials that stabilize five or more distinct cation species on a single crystallographic sublattice through configurational entropy. The resulting chemical complexity — with every magnetic site surrounded by a statistically random local environment — creates a rich playground for disorder-driven magnetic phenomena that are fundamentally different from those in chemically ordered or lightly-doped magnets.

My research characterizes the magnetic landscape of transition-metal HEOs using neutron and X-ray diffraction together with total scattering and pair distribution function (PDF) analysis. By tracking how scattering signatures evolve with temperature and applied field, we distinguish conventional spin-glass freezing from genuinely frustrated collective states that arise from the interplay of random exchange, single-ion anisotropy distributions, and geometric constraints imposed by the lattice topology.

Experimental Approach

  • Neutron powder diffraction for long-range magnetic structure determination
  • Total neutron scattering and PDF analysis for local structural correlations
  • X-ray total scattering for cation distribution and strain-field mapping
  • Temperature- and field-dependent magnetometry to anchor structural results
  • Reverse Monte Carlo (RMC) modelling to build chemically-consistent supercell models

Central Questions

  • Does cation disorder in HEOs produce spin-glass freezing, or can it stabilize a genuinely frustrated collective ground state?
  • How do local structural distortions (bond-length variance, cation size mismatch) couple to the distribution of magnetic exchange interactions?
  • Can the cation composition be tuned to shift the balance between competing exchange pathways and achieve desired magnetic functionality?
  • What are the spectroscopic fingerprints that unambiguously distinguish random-bond glass states from correlated disorder in scattering experiments?

Disorder and Magnetism in Multi-Cation Oxides

In conventional dilute magnets, disorder is treated perturbatively — a few impurities perturb an otherwise ordered host. In HEOs, disorder is the ground state: every magnetic ion sits in a unique local environment defined by the random arrangement of its neighbors. The distribution of crystal-field splittings, exchange integrals, and anisotropy axes is therefore intrinsic rather than extrinsic.

We focus on rock-salt and spinel HEOs with 3d transition metals (Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Zn) occupying the magnetic sublattice. Neutron diffraction resolves whether long-range magnetic order survives despite cation randomness, while PDF analysis captures the short-range correlations — including chemical short-range order (CSRO) — that are invisible to Bragg scattering. These local correlations are crucial: small departures from perfect random mixing create correlated exchange-pathway networks that can support frustration-induced collective behavior well below the nominal glass-freezing temperature.

Scattering-Based Characterization Strategy

A key challenge in HEO magnetism is that macroscopic susceptibility and specific-heat measurements cannot reliably distinguish spin-glass states from highly frustrated ordered phases — both show broad features and history dependence. Direct scattering measurements offer a decisive advantage: diffuse magnetic scattering encodes the spatial correlations of spin fluctuations, while its evolution with temperature and wavevector reveals the characteristic length and time scales of the freezing process.

Our approach combines temperature-resolved neutron total scattering (sensitive to both structural and magnetic diffuse scattering) with X-ray total scattering (which provides contrast for the cation distribution via anomalous scattering). By building RMC supercell models consistent with both data sets simultaneously, we reconstruct chemically and magnetically realistic structural ensembles from which thermodynamic quantities can be computed and compared directly to bulk measurements.

Toward Designed Disordered Magnets

The ultimate goal of this research line is to use the vast composition space of HEOs as a design lever — moving beyond passive characterization toward predictive control of magnetic functionality. Statistical and mean-field models that map cation configurations to effective exchange parameters can, in principle, guide synthesis toward compositions that maximize frustration, optimize magnetocaloric response, or enhance spin-glass robustness against perturbations.

We are currently developing composition–property relationships that connect measurable scattering features (diffuse peak positions, correlation lengths, PDF peak widths) to ensemble-averaged microscopic parameters, providing a route to rational design of disordered magnetic oxides for applications in data storage, neuromorphic computing, and low-field cooling technologies.
