Profile

About Me

I am an experimental condensed-matter physicist, currently a postdoctoral fellow at the Stewart Blusson Quantum Matter Institute (SBQMI) at the University of British Columbia in Vancouver. Most of my work happens at neutron and X-ray scattering facilities, where I study how spins, the lattice, and crystal-field states talk to each other — and what happens to that conversation when a material is pushed by disorder, magnetic field, pressure, or chemical substitution.

The questions I keep returning to are about collective excitations: how they form in ordered and unconventional magnets, why they hybridize, and how tuning a material reshapes its magnetic spectrum. I try to anchor every model in what the data actually show, with the longer-term aim of finding the organizing principles behind magnetic behavior in complex quantum materials.

Research

Current Research

Altermagnetism & Magnon-Phonon Coupling

Altermagnets order magnetically without any net magnetization, yet their energy bands are spin-split — a combination that fits neither the ferromagnetic nor the antiferromagnetic mold. I study the spin dynamics of three such materials. In α-MnTe, I resolved the spin-split magnon dispersion and followed how doping and pressure rework the magnetic order and its coupling to phonons. In hematite, I am after the chiral magnon branches that the altermagnetic symmetry predicts. And in RuO₂, whose magnetic ground state remains contested, our inelastic neutron scattering and Mössbauer measurements set the tightest bounds on the magnetism published so far. The experiments run through a PI-led neutron user program on SEQUOIA, ARCS, HYSPEC, and TAX at SNS and HFIR.

Magnon Resilience in Diluted Antiferromagnets

Dilute an antiferromagnet with enough non-magnetic atoms and its spin waves should eventually fall apart — percolation theory even says when. In Y-doped TbSb, they don't. As yttrium replaces terbium, the magnon branches stay well-defined far beyond the point where the textbook picture predicts collapse. The extra stability seems to come from crystal-field levels hybridizing with the collective spin-wave modes. With measurements on HYSPEC, ARCS, and VERITAS at SNS, I want to pin down that mechanism — and, more broadly, learn how local single-ion anisotropy shields collective magnetism from disorder.

Nanoengineered Artificial Honeycomb Lattices

For my PhD, I patterned thin-film permalloy and Nd into honeycomb networks to watch how emergent magnetic charges behave when the lattice geometry is controlled at the nanoscale. The Nd lattices gave us a surprise: a quantum-disordered state of those charges, which ended up as the Frontispiece of Advanced Science (2021). In the permalloy networks, the same charge physics rectifies — the array behaves as a magnetic diode with an unusually low forward voltage, tunable by field history and temperature (Mater. Today Phys., 2022). Much of the evidence came from polarized neutron reflectometry and SANS with DWBA modeling, techniques I learned hands-on at the ORNL beamlines.

Itinerant Magnets & Quantum Information

At SBQMI, I work on itinerant magnets that never settle into conventional order. Their spin fluctuations spread across wide ranges of energy and wavevector, and the correlations buried in those fluctuations are exactly what standard probes have trouble quantifying. My approach is to pull the Quantum Fisher Information (QFI) straight out of neutron scattering data — QFI bounds the multipartite entanglement from below, which ties a scattering experiment to quantities that matter for quantum sensing and computing. In parallel, I grow single crystals and characterize their bulk properties to decide which systems deserve beam time. What I am really after are materials whose magnetic fluctuations can do useful quantum-information work.

Mentorship

Teaching & Mentoring

As a postdoc, I usually come on board once a project has a concrete materials or scattering question to answer. I am not in a position to hire students myself right now, but I gladly co-advise, mentor students through neutron and X-ray experiments, and team up on joint proposals. When I teach — at the college or graduate level — I build up from fundamentals rather than down from formalism.
