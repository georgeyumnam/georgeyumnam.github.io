Quantum Magnetism

Reduced dimensionality · Spin-liquid proximate phases · Neutron scattering & numerics

Overview

Quantum magnetism occupies the frontier where classical spin-wave theory breaks down: in low-dimensional, strongly frustrated, or anisotropically coupled magnets, quantum fluctuations are large enough to melt conventional ordered phases and stabilize states characterized by long-range entanglement rather than symmetry breaking. These quantum spin liquids and related exotic ordered phases are among the most challenging and consequential problems in condensed matter physics — both as fundamental puzzles in many-body quantum mechanics and as potential platforms for topological quantum computation.

My research focuses on identifying and characterizing chemically accessible model systems where proximate spin-liquid physics, fractionalized excitations, and multipolar order can be detected with neutron scattering. By combining elastic and inelastic neutron scattering with numerical techniques — exact diagonalization, density-matrix renormalization group (DMRG), and linear spin-wave theory — we move between experiment and theory to construct a coherent picture of the quantum ground state and its excitations.

Experimental & Numerical Toolkit

  • Elastic neutron diffraction for long-range order parameter determination
  • Inelastic neutron scattering (INS) on powder and single-crystal samples
  • Diffuse scattering for mapping short-range spin correlations in disordered phases
  • Exact diagonalization (ED) on finite clusters for comparison to measured spectra
  • DMRG and linear spin-wave theory for quasi-1D and 2D model Hamiltonians
  • SpinW and SPINVERT codes for spin-wave and reverse Monte Carlo modelling

Central Questions

  • How do competing exchange interactions (frustration) and reduced dimensionality conspire to suppress long-range order and stabilize quantum spin-liquid proximate phases?
  • What are the spectroscopic fingerprints — in neutron spectra — that unambiguously distinguish a spin liquid from a disordered magnet?
  • Can multipolar order (quadrupolar, octupolar) be stabilized in spin–orbit entangled magnets beyond the dipole-ordered ground states typically studied?
  • How does quenched disorder (vacancies, bond randomness) renormalize the exotic phases and their excitations?

Frustration and Quantum Fluctuations

Classical frustration arises when the lattice geometry prevents all interactions from being simultaneously satisfied — the canonical example being antiferromagnetic interactions on a triangular lattice. In the quantum case, the situation is richer: zero-point fluctuations of the spins can select an ordered state from a classically degenerate manifold (order by disorder), suppress order entirely and produce a resonating-valence-bond spin liquid, or generate exotic orders involving higher multipole moments that are invisible to conventional magnetic probes.

We study materials on geometrically frustrated lattices — honeycomb, triangular, kagome, and pyrochlore — where the interplay between exchange anisotropy, Kitaev-type bond-directional interactions, and Dzyaloshinskii–Moriya terms creates a rich competition between candidate ground states. Neutron scattering is uniquely positioned to discriminate between these phases: the wavevector and energy dependence of the magnetic scattering encodes the real-space spin correlations and the quasiparticle dispersion, both of which are highly sensitive to the specific ground state.

Spin-Liquid Proximate Phases and Fractionalization

Even when a material does order magnetically, it may do so in the vicinity of a quantum spin-liquid phase — a "proximate" spin liquid. In this regime, the excitation spectrum retains signatures of fractionalized quasiparticles (spinons, Majorana fermions, visons) even though the ground state breaks time-reversal symmetry. Experimentally, these signatures appear as broad continua in the inelastic neutron spectrum at energies above the magnon branches, persisting well into the ordered phase. Distinguishing these continua from magnon-magnon scattering or phonon contributions is a core methodological challenge that we address by combining temperature and field evolution of the spectrum with numerical benchmarks.

We are particularly interested in spin–orbit-coupled magnets based on 4d and 5d transition metals (Ru, Ir, Os) and f-electron systems (Ce, Yb, Pr), where the effective spin-1/2 moments carry strong bond-directional anisotropy that can realize Kitaev-type Hamiltonians. In these systems, the combination of frustrated geometry and highly anisotropic exchange places the ground state precariously close to a quantum phase transition, making them ideal laboratories for quantum spin-liquid physics at accessible energy scales.

From Quantum Magnets to Quantum Technology

Beyond their fundamental interest, quantum magnets offer tangible technological leverage. Topological spin-liquid phases host anyonic excitations whose non-Abelian braiding statistics could underpin fault-tolerant quantum gates. Entangled ground states in frustrated magnets exhibit non-classical correlations that can be harvested for quantum sensing and quantum simulation. And the tunability of exchange interactions in molecular magnets and van der Waals magnetic materials — combined with the spatial resolution of scanning probe and X-ray microscopy techniques — opens the possibility of engineering quantum spin Hamiltonians at the nanoscale.

Our longer-term goal is to identify systems where the gap between the exotic ground state and the first excited states is large enough to be practically exploitable — robust against thermal fluctuations and disorder — while remaining within the reach of existing synthesis and nanofabrication capabilities. By building quantitative models from neutron scattering data, we aim to provide theoretically grounded design rules for this next generation of quantum magnetic materials.
