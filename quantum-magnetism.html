Itinerant Magnets & Quantum Information

Correlated spin fluctuations · Quantum Fisher Information · Quantum sensing

Overview

At SBQMI, I am studying itinerant magnets that refuse to order in any conventional way. Their spin fluctuations sprawl across energy and wavevector, and the quantum correlations they carry are spread over many degrees of freedom rather than locked into an ordered pattern. Standard probes struggle here: the inelastic neutron spectra come out broad and diffuse, and it is tempting — too tempting — to file them under "disorder" or "short-range fluctuations" and move on.

The centerpiece of this program is turning Quantum Fisher Information (QFI) into a practical, working tool for reading entanglement out of neutron scattering structure factors. QFI gives a rigorous lower bound on multipartite entanglement, and it can be computed from the data without assuming anything about the ground state. That puts neutron measurements in direct conversation with quantum information science — and provides a concrete, experiment-based yardstick for whether a material's quantum correlations are strong enough to matter for sensing or computing.

Research Approach

  • Single-crystal synthesis and physical property characterization (PPMS/MPMS) to screen candidate itinerant quantum magnets
  • Inelastic neutron scattering at SNS and HFIR to measure dynamic structure factors over wide energy-momentum ranges
  • Extraction of Quantum Fisher Information (QFI) from measured neutron scattering intensities via frequency sum rules
  • Comparison of QFI bounds with theoretical entanglement measures for candidate ground state models
  • Temperature- and field-dependent measurements to map how entanglement content evolves with external tuning parameters

Central Questions

  • Which itinerant magnets host the strongest multipartite entanglement as quantified by QFI from neutron data?
  • How does entanglement content evolve with temperature, field, and chemical tuning — and can it be sustained at accessible temperatures?
  • Can QFI-based diagnostics distinguish genuinely correlated paramagnetic states from conventional disorder-broadened phases?
  • What material properties — bandwidth, frustration, spin-orbit coupling — are most conducive to sustaining high QFI?

Quantum Fisher Information from Neutron Scattering

The QFI density FQ follows from the dynamic spin structure factor S(q, ω) through an integral over frequency at fixed wavevector. Because of this sum rule, QFI comes straight from inelastic neutron data — no model input required. And the certification is sharp: whenever FQ/N exceeds k, at least k+1 spins in the system are genuinely entangled with one another.

The practical demands are wide energy-transfer coverage and careful absolute normalization, both within reach of the modern time-of-flight spectrometers at SNS. I am working toward a reliable, repeatable protocol for QFI extraction across different classes of quantum magnets, so the resulting entanglement benchmarks can tell us which materials are worth serious pursuit for quantum information applications.

Crystal Synthesis and Material Screening

Most itinerant magnets will not host strong quantum correlations, and there is no shortcut for finding the ones that do. The interesting cases are those where kinetic energy, exchange, and geometric frustration conspire to produce a strongly correlated paramagnet — rather than simply ordering, or going quietly paramagnetic. I grow single crystals of the candidates at SBQMI and characterize their bulk magnetic and thermodynamic behavior with PPMS and MPMS.

Susceptibility, specific heat, and resistivity versus temperature and field sketch the first map of each phase diagram and flag anomalous correlations worth a closer look. Screening this way means the neutron beam time — always the scarcest resource — goes to the most promising materials instead of routine surveys.

Connection to Quantum Technology

The link to quantum technology is unusually direct here. QFI is precisely the quantity that sets the sensitivity of a quantum sensor at the Heisenberg limit — the fundamental bound on measurement precision. A material carrying high multipartite entanglement in its spin fluctuations could serve as a quantum-enhanced medium for detecting weak magnetic fields, in a regime where classical sensors run into shot noise. Finding magnets that reach high QFI at practical temperatures and fields is a real step toward quantum materials with technological consequences.
