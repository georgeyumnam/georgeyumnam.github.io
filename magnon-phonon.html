Altermagnetism & Magnon-Phonon Coupling

Spin–lattice coupling · Inelastic neutron scattering · Thermal transport

Overview

Textbooks tend to treat spin waves (magnons) and lattice vibrations (phonons) as separate channels. Real materials rarely cooperate: spin–orbit coupling, exchange striction, and magnetoelastic interactions mix the two, and the resulting hybrid quasiparticles behave in ways neither parent mode does. I use inelastic neutron scattering (INS) and resonant inelastic X-ray scattering (RIXS) to watch this hybridization directly, resolving how magnon branches couple to optical phonons in itinerant antiferromagnets and correlated insulators.

Tracking how dispersions renormalize, linewidths broaden, and spectral weight shifts as functions of momentum, energy, temperature, and field lets us extract the microscopic spin–lattice coupling constants at play. Those constants then go into model Hamiltonians, so the hybrid spectrum can be connected to quantities measurable in bulk: thermal conductivity, the spin Seebeck coefficient, and magnon lifetimes.

Experimental Techniques

  • Time-of-flight inelastic neutron scattering (SEQUOIA, ARCS, HYSPEC, TAX at SNS/HFIR)
  • Resonant inelastic X-ray scattering (RIXS) at synchrotron beamlines
  • Nuclear resonant inelastic X-ray scattering (NRIXS) and Mössbauer spectroscopy
  • Neutron powder and single-crystal diffraction for structural characterization
  • Ab initio lattice dynamics (DFT-DFPT) for phonon benchmarking

Key Scientific Questions

  • Under what conditions does spin–lattice coupling produce avoided crossings versus broadened continua in the excitation spectrum?
  • How does chemical or structural disorder tune the magnon–phonon hybridization strength?
  • Can selective coupling to specific phonon branches enhance or suppress spin-heat conversion efficiency?
  • What are the signatures of magnon–phonon coupling in transport observables, and can they be predicted from scattering data alone?

Case Study: Magnon Gap Tuning in Li-doped MnTe

Hexagonal MnTe is an altermagnetic semiconductor whose large magnon gap is set by single-ion anisotropy and exchange. On the HYSPEC and ARCS spectrometers at the Spallation Neutron Source (SNS, ORNL), we measured the full magnon dispersion across a series of LixMn1−xTe samples spanning a wide doping range. Putting Li on the Mn site does two things at once: it dopes holes into the system, and it distorts the local structure, changing the crystal-field environment of the remaining Mn²⁺ ions.

The magnon gap turned out to track the Li content smoothly and systematically, growing by more than 50% at moderate doping, while the long-range antiferromagnetic order survived intact. Comparing the measured dispersion against spin-wave theory with a magnetoelastic term showed that the gap moves mainly because spin–lattice interaction renormalizes the single-ion anisotropy; the exchange integrals barely change. Li-doped MnTe is, in effect, a working example of magnon-gap engineering by chemistry.

Case Study: Lattice Dynamics and Magnetism in RuO₂

RuO₂ has drawn intense attention as a candidate altermagnet: no net magnetization, but spin-split bands that would make it valuable for spin transport. Whether it actually orders magnetically, and by how much, is still debated; different probes have returned conflicting answers. We came at the question from the lattice side instead, combining phonon spectroscopy (NRIXS) with Mössbauer spectroscopy to constrain the magnetic and electronic state independently of the usual magnetic probes.

NRIXS resolves the ⁵⁷Fe partial phonon density of states in Fe-doped RuO₂ at high energy resolution, fine enough to catch the small force-constant shifts that magnetic ordering would leave behind. Together with the Mössbauer hyperfine parameters, the data put quantitative upper bounds on any ordered moment in RuO₂ and constrain how correlated the electrons can be. The work also demonstrates that phonon probes can act as precision sensors for magnetism where conventional magnetometry gives ambiguous answers.

Broader Implications

Magnon–phonon hybridization is not just spectroscopic fine print; it shapes how magnonic and thermoelectric devices would actually perform. Strong spin–lattice coupling opens avoided crossings that can gate spin-wave propagation by frequency, and the same coupling alters phonon lifetimes, and with them thermal conductivity, in ways purely electronic models miss. What we contribute is the quantitative microscopic input, taken directly from scattering data, that turns these effects from something one observes into something one can engineer.
